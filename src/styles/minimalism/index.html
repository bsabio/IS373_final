Minimalism

The art of intentional simplicity, where every element earns its place.

Description

Minimalism focuses on simplicity and clarity, removing anything that does not support the main message. It emphasizes whitespace, neutral colors, and quiet typography to create balance and calm. The design feels clean, spacious, and intentional, with very few visual elements.

Historical Background

Minimalism emerged from modernist art and architecture from the 1960s and 1970s, influenced by movements like De Stijl, Bauhaus, and Japanese Zen philosophy. Designers such as Dieter Rams championed the principle "less, but better," rejecting decoration in favor of functional purity.

As software evolved, minimalism shaped digital interfaces by prioritizing usability and reduction instead of visual noise.

Color & Typography Rules

• White or black base with large negative space

• One accent color at most

• Thin or neutral sans‑serif typefaces

• Simple geometric layout and clear hierarchy

• No decorative effects or texture

"Perfection is achieved not when there is nothing more to add, but when there is nothing left to take away." Antoine de Saint-Exupéry
